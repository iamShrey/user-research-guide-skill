# Questioning Rulebook for User Research Interviews

This rulebook captures the core principles for formulating rigorous, unbiased interview questions. Apply these rules when drafting and reviewing every question in a guide. The goal is to create conditions where participants tell you what they actually think and do — not what they think you want to hear.

---

## Rule 1: Open Before You Close

Use open-ended questions as your default. Reserve closed (yes/no, scale, multiple-choice) questions for closing confirmations or demographic framing only.

**Why this matters:** Open questions invite participants to define the space themselves. Closed questions hand them a predetermined frame and you learn their reaction to your frame, not their native experience.

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Use instead |
|---|---|
| "Do you find it hard to track your tasks?" | "How do you currently keep track of your tasks?" |
| "Is collaboration a problem for your team?" | "Tell me about a time your team needed to coordinate on something. How did that go?" |
| "Would you use a feature like this?" | "Where does this fit into how you currently work?" |

---

## Rule 2: Ask About Behavior Before Attitude

Ask what people do before asking what they think or feel. Behavior is more reliable data than opinion.

**Why this matters:** Attitudes are rationalizations. Behavior is evidence. "I think I'd use that" is much weaker signal than "I currently solve this problem by doing X." Anchor the conversation in real episodes before exploring beliefs.

**The sequence that works:**
1. "Walk me through the last time you [did the relevant thing]." (specific episode)
2. "What happened? What did you use?" (behavior)
3. "How did that feel?" / "What was frustrating about that?" (attitude, grounded in experience)

---

## Rule 3: One Question at a Time

Never bundle two questions into one. Pick the most important one and ask it. Ask the second one only if the first answer doesn't cover it.

**Why this matters:** Double-barreled questions let participants answer the easier half and skip the harder half. You lose data.

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Use instead |
|---|---|
| "What do you find most challenging about this workflow, and how do you usually deal with it?" | "What's the most challenging part of this workflow for you?" (then follow up: "How do you usually deal with that?") |

---

## Rule 4: No Leading Language

Remove any word or phrase that signals a desired or expected answer. This includes:
- Presuppositions ("When you're feeling overwhelmed by notifications...")
- Loaded descriptors ("painful", "broken", "annoying" — unless quoting back the participant)
- Positive framing that implies endorsement ("this great new feature...")
- Hedging that implies the expected answer is negative ("have you ever struggled with...")

**Test:** Could a reasonable person read the question and infer what answer you're looking for? If yes, rewrite it.

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Use instead |
|---|---|
| "How frustrating is it when the system goes down?" | "What happens for you when the system is unavailable?" |
| "Do you agree that the current process is too slow?" | "How would you describe the speed of the current process?" |
| "What do you like most about [feature]?" | "Tell me about your experience with [feature]." |

---

## Rule 5: Assume Nothing

Don't assume the participant has experienced the behavior you're asking about. Use conditional framing when needed.

**Why this matters:** If a participant hasn't done something, a direct question about it forces them to either admit ignorance (embarrassing) or fabricate an answer (misleading).

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Use instead |
|---|---|
| "How do you decide when to escalate an issue?" | "Can you think of a time you needed to escalate something? If so, how did you decide to do that?" |
| "What do you use to share files with clients?" | "How do you typically handle sharing files or documents when you need to? Does that come up for you?" |

---

## Rule 6: Ladder for Motivation

For any answer that reveals a behavior or preference, follow up with "why" to uncover the underlying motivation. Keep laddering until you reach a human need or a genuine constraint.

**The ladder sequence:**
1. Participant states a behavior or preference
2. "What makes that important to you?" (or "Why does that matter?")
3. "And why is that?" (repeat 2-3 times)
4. You'll often arrive at something like: time, status, safety, autonomy, trust

This is where the most actionable product insight lives.

**Example:**
- Participant: "I always send a follow-up email after the meeting."
- You: "What makes you do that?"
- Participant: "So people don't forget what we agreed."
- You: "Why is that important to you?"
- Participant: "If they forget, I end up doing all the work myself."
- *Root need:* Fairness / accountability

---

## Rule 7: Use the Pause

After a participant answers, wait 3–5 seconds before speaking. Silence is uncomfortable and people fill it — often with their most honest and interesting thoughts.

Include the note **[pause]** in the guide at moments where a rich answer is especially valuable.

---

## Rule 8: Probe for Specificity

When a participant gives a vague or general answer, probe for a specific example. Generalizations are often rationalizations; specific stories are evidence.

**Probing phrases to include in guides:**
- "Can you give me a specific example of that?"
- "Tell me about the last time that happened."
- "What did that actually look like in practice?"
- "Can you walk me through that?"

---

## Rule 9: Emotionally Sensitive Topics

If the research touches on frustration, failure, workarounds, or negative experiences, approach carefully:
- Start with a neutral framing: "What was that experience like for you?" rather than "Was that difficult?"
- Give the participant an easy out: "Some people find this easy, others find it tricky — what's your experience?"
- Never dwell in pain; balance with "what has worked well for you in that situation?"

---

## Rule 10: Question Length

Keep questions short. A good interview question is rarely more than 20 words. If you need more words to make it unambiguous, the question is probably doing too much — split it up.

A useful check: can a non-expert read the question aloud smoothly on their first try? If not, simplify.

---

## Applying the Rulebook

When reviewing a draft guide, go question by question and check:
- [ ] Open-ended? (Rule 1)
- [ ] Behavior before attitude? (Rule 2)
- [ ] Single question only? (Rule 3)
- [ ] No leading language or loaded words? (Rule 4)
- [ ] No assumed behaviors? (Rule 5)
- [ ] Ladder/probe included for the most important questions? (Rule 6)
- [ ] Pause note where appropriate? (Rule 7)
- [ ] Specific example prompt available? (Rule 8)
- [ ] Sensitive topics handled carefully? (Rule 9)
- [ ] Under 20 words? (Rule 10)
